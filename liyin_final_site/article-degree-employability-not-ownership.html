A Degree Helps Employability, Not Ownership

A degree can improve employability, but that is different from building ownership, leverage, and compounding outcomes.

A degree can improve employability, but that is different from building ownership, leverage, and compounding outcomes.

Why this matters

This matters because many young people are taught to treat education and value creation as the same thing. They are related, but not identical. A degree can help you qualify for opportunities, but qualification is not the same as control, upside, or ownership.

What most people get wrong

The common mistake is assuming that a safer path automatically compounds. It may increase your chances of getting hired, but hiring still means trading time for compensation inside someone else’s system. That can be useful, but it is not the same as building assets, equity, audience, intellectual property, or systems that continue to work when you are not present.

My framework

My framework is simple: employability gives you access; ownership gives you upside. Employability improves your ability to enter a market. Ownership changes your share of the future value created in that market. Both matter, but they solve different problems.

What this means for young people

For young people, this means you do not need to reject a degree. You need to understand its limits. Use it as a platform, not a full identity. Learn how to get hired, but also learn how value is captured, why incentives matter, and what kinds of assets can compound outside a paycheck.

My current conclusion

I do not think the degree is useless. I think it is incomplete. It can help you enter the game, but it does not automatically teach you how ownership, leverage, or compounding really work.
